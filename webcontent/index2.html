Brief History about World Cup in Brazil

The FIFA Football World Cup is an international sporting event which takes place once in every four years where 32 teams battle it out for the coveted prize, the World Cup trophy.

FIFA World Cup 2014 is the 20th edition of the competition which began way back in 1930.

The 2014 edition is being hosted by Brazil, five time champions of the world. This is the second time the event is being organised in Brazil, the previous one being in 1950 in which Brazil finished runners up behind Uruguay.

The final of the 1950 World Cup was held in the Maracana stadium, Rio De Janeiro in which 173,850 fans witnessed Uruguay beat the home team Brazil 2-1.

The recorded 173,850 attendance was the highest ever for a football match.

This year also the final will be held in the same Maracana stadium which underwent renovations leading up to the tournament.

Brazil have won this tournament five times, more than any other country, their most recent win being in 2002 when the tournament was held for the first time in Asia in South Korea and Japan.

Brazil's last affair with hosting the World Cup
Brazilians love their Samba. They love it to the extent that it can be seen in the style of football they play. Right from Pele, then Zico to Ronaldinho, we have seen so many Brazilian stars dancing their way around opponent defenders as if doing a samba. They call it the Brazilian way, artistes of the highest quality.

The World Cup 2014 is arriving to Brazil for the second time in history, after 64 years. The previous edition that was held in Brazil was way back in 1950. The Brazilian players of the time, rich in skill and artistry, terrorized defenders scoring as many goals to reach the final in front of the home fans. The final was to be held in the Maracana stadium, Rio De Janeiro, the same Maracana stadium which will host the final of 2014. The wily Brazilians were set to meet Uruguay, who had scrapped through to the final, drawing 2-2 with Spain and only just winning 3-2 against Sweden.

Few days before the game, the Brazilian press declared Brazil as Champions. There was a general consensus among the public that Brazil were set for a landslide victory. That morning, the streets of Rio were bustling with activity. Brazilian supporters gathered for an improvised carnival organised to celebrate the crowning champions.

Brazil lined up in their all white uniform along with Uruguay before kick-off. Rumour has it that during the Brazilian national anthem, a Uruguayan player urinated, intimidated by the reverberations made by the Brazilian supporters. 173,850 fans (the highest ever attendance recorded for a sports match) were packed inside the Maracana to see the crowning glory of their dear team. But Uruguayan captain, Obdulio Varela had other ideas.

The Brazilian newspaper O Mundo printed an early edition on the day of the final containing a photograph of Brazil with the caption "These are the world champions". Obdulio Varela bought as many copies as he could, laid them on his bathroom floor and encouraged his teammates to urinate on them. Moments before the game, the Uruguayan coach Juan López informed his team that their best chance of surviving the powerful offensive line of Brazil would come through adopting a defensive strategy. After he left, Varela stood up and addressed the team himself, saying, "Juancito is a good man, but today, he is wrong. If we play defensively against Brazil, our fate will be no different from Spain or Sweden". He gave an emotional speech inspiring his team mates, encouraging them not to be intimidated by the opposition and the fans.

The game began as expected, Brazil, with their fluid, free flowing attack which Uruguay somehow coped in the first half and the score was goalless. But right after the interval, Brazil scored just two minutes into the second half. The crowd went berserk. Obdulio Varela questioned the legitimacy of the goal intentionally, in such a manner that the English referee had to bring out an interpreter. During this argument the crowd cooled down. Varela took the ball and placed it at the centre, and shouted to his team in a macho style, "Now, its time we win".

Uruguayans were able to take control of the game and Schiaffino scored in the 66th minute. Then just 11 minutes from time, Alcides Ghiggia came running from the right flank and scored Uruguay's second goal. The crowd was dumbfounded. The Maracana sounded completely empty, even with the near 200,000 people inside it. Brazilian players were stunned. They had gotten so carried away that they never prepared a Plan B for when they trailed. Brazilian players had forgotten their skills and tricks.

The referee blew the final whistle, and Brazil had lost. The Brazilian government had already made arrangements, in advance for the presentation ceremony for Brazil. Gold medals with Brazilian player names were made, at a time when FIFA did not hand out medals to teams. Now that Uruguay had won, there was no ceremony prepared. Amidst the morbid silence, the FIFA President Jules Rimmet who was left all alone with the trophy, found Varela and handed it out to him on the pitch. The crowd stood in disbelief, they were stripped off the title they had already considered rightfully theirs.

The aftermath of this match was so devastating that Brazil changed their kit colour from all white to the colours of their flag, as they considered it to be a bad omen. Several players silently walked into retirement, while others were forced out of the side. They say the pain and anguish of the 200,000 odd supporters can be felt in the empty Maracana stadium even to this day.
